# Problem Mining

## Overview

The best products come from people who already solved a problem — they just
haven't packaged it yet. Most people skip straight to building and wonder why
nobody buys. This skill prevents that by interviewing the user to extract
problems they've *already* solved, validating those problems against what
people actually pay for, and synthesizing concrete product ideas from the
strongest candidates.

The interview is always **one question at a time**. Never batch questions.
The value is focus — each answer shapes the next question.

## When to use

- "I want to build something but don't know what"
- "What should I productize?"
- "I have skills but no product idea"
- "Help me find a problem worth solving"
- "What could I sell?"
- Any variant of "I don't know which problems are worth solving"

## Core principle

> You almost certainly have already fixed problems worth productizing.
> It's damn near impossible to live into adulthood without doing so.

The alternative — solving problems you *think* exist from the outside — is a
gamble. You don't understand the market, and you risk building something
nobody wants. Start with what you've lived.

## Interview structure

The interview has **four phases**, each building on the last. Complete each
phase before advancing. Adjust questions based on prior answers — some
become moot, some become load-bearing.

### Phase 1: Achievement extraction

**Goal:** Build a raw list of everything the user has achieved, no matter
how small.

Start with a warm-up question, then probe specific categories one at a time.
Don't present the category list upfront — ask about each area naturally so
nothing gets overlooked.

Probe areas (ask about each separately):

- Times they made money doing something specific
- Systems they built that saved significant time
- Problems they talked through with someone and then overcame
- Online communities where they helped others achieve something
- Things they built from scratch that worked
- Major obstacles they overcame that others still struggle with
- Results they get that people around them can't

For each achievement surfaced, capture it in the user's own words. Push for
specifics — "signed 3 clients" beats "got some clients."

**Exit criterion:** User has listed at least 3–5 concrete achievements with
enough detail to analyze. If they stall, prompt with examples:

> "signed 3 clients to my YT growth agency"
> "built an AI automation that saved 10 hours/week"
> "went from 0 to 5,000 email subscribers with paid traffic"

### Phase 2: Problem decomposition

**Goal:** For each achievement, extract the problem that was overcome.

Take each achievement from Phase 1 and ask (one at a time):

1. What blocked you initially?
2. What did you struggle with for months before the breakthrough?
3. What was the breakthrough that changed everything?
4. What do others still struggle with that you figured out?

Capture the problem-solution pair:

```
Achievement: [what they achieved]
Initial blocker: [what stopped them]
Breakthrough: [what changed everything]
Others still stuck on: [where peers remain blocked]
```

**Exit criterion:** Every achievement has a decomposed problem-solution pair.

### Phase 3: Desire mapping

**Goal:** Filter to problems that trace back to core desires.

People pay to solve problems connected to four things:

| Desire | Signal |
|---|---|
| Making money | Solving this directly helps someone earn more |
| Saving money | Solving this reduces meaningful costs |
| Saving time | Solving this reclaims significant hours |
| Status / attraction | Solving this improves how others perceive them |

For each problem from Phase 2, ask:

> "When you solved [problem], did it primarily help you make money, save
> money, save time, or improve your status/reputation? Be specific about
> the impact."

**Kill anything that doesn't trace back to at least one core desire.** Tell
the user directly — "this one is interesting but it's a 'nice to have,' not
something people pay for" — and remove it from the working list.

**Exit criterion:** Remaining problems all connect to money, time, or
status with a clear explanation of how.

### Phase 4: Root-cause reframing

**Goal:** Separate surface problems from root problems.

For each surviving problem, ask:

> "When someone has this problem, what do they *think* their problem is?
> And what's actually going on underneath?"

Capture both layers:

```
Surface (what they think): "I need more clients"
Root (what it actually is): "I don't know how to position myself as an
authority so ideal clients come to me"
```

Explain the principle: *You sell the surface problem but solve the root
problem.* The surface is what gets attention; the root is what delivers
transformation.

**Exit criterion:** Every problem has both a surface and root articulation.

## Post-interview validation

After all four phases, run three checks on each remaining problem. Present
findings to the user, don't just ask — analyze their answers and give your
assessment.

### Check 1: Pain intensity

> Is this problem painful enough that people will pay to fix it?

"Mildly annoying" problems don't create buyers. "I'd lose a kidney for
this" problems do. Assess based on what the user described in Phase 2 — how
long they struggled, how much it cost them, how emotional the breakthrough
was.

### Check 2: Transformation clarity

Ask the user to complete this sentence for each problem:

> "The [PROBLEM] was costing me [PAIN]. I discovered [SOLUTION], and now
> [OUTCOME]."

If they can't articulate it simply in one sentence, they don't understand it
well enough yet. Flag it — don't kill it, but note the gap.

### Check 3: Unfair advantage audit

For each validated problem, confirm the user has:

| Advantage | Question |
|---|---|
| Authenticity | Did you personally live through this? |
| Proof | Do you have tangible results to show? |
| Understanding | Can you describe the pain in a way that makes someone say "that's exactly it"? |
| Head start | Have you already solved it for yourself? |

Score each problem on these four dimensions. Problems with all four are
prime candidates. Problems missing one or more need the gap addressed
before productizing.

## Synthesis: Product ideas

After validation, produce a **Product Ideas Brief** containing:

### For each validated problem (ranked by strength):

1. **The problem** — surface version and root version
2. **The transformation** — the one-sentence before/after
3. **Core desire** — which of the four it maps to
4. **Unfair advantage score** — which of the four advantages the user has
5. **Product concepts** — 2–3 concrete ways to productize this:
   - What format? (course, template, tool, service, community, SaaS)
   - Who's the buyer? (specific persona, not "everyone")
   - What's the price signal? (based on pain intensity and desire mapping)
   - What's the MVP? (smallest thing to validate demand)
6. **Risk** — what could make this fail, and what would you need to learn first

### Overall recommendation

Rank the problems and make a clear recommendation:

- **Best bet:** The problem with the strongest combination of pain, proof,
  and market signal
- **Dark horse:** A problem that scored lower but has unusually strong
  positioning potential
- **Kill list:** Problems that didn't survive validation, with clear reasons

## Escape hatch

If the user genuinely cannot surface any solved problems after thorough
interviewing, acknowledge it directly — no fake encouragement. Then offer
two paths:

1. **Community immersion:** Get involved in communities with shared problems
   you're facing. Consume content, learn solutions, solve the problem, then
   return to this skill.
2. **Affiliate-first:** Search affiliate networks for products solving
   real problems. Do a deep dive on the problem space and start selling
   existing solutions while building domain expertise.

Both paths lead back to this interview once the user has lived experience.

## Interview style

- **One question at a time.** Always. The value is focus.
- **Use their language.** Mirror back their words, not business jargon.
- **Push for specifics.** "Got some clients" → "How many? What kind? How?"
- **Be direct about kills.** If a problem doesn't pass a filter, say so
  plainly. Don't let weak ideas survive out of politeness.
- **Show your work.** When analyzing, explain why you're scoring something
  the way you are. The user should learn the framework, not just get output.
- **Keep a running tally.** After each phase, summarize what's on the board
  before moving forward. The user should always know where they stand.
