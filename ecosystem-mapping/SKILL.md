# Ecosystem Mapping

## Overview

Most people build products linearly: pick a format (course, SaaS, service),
build it, market it, maybe bolt on an upsell. One product, one revenue
stream, one type of customer.

The alternative: your solution is the **core transformation** you deliver.
Your products are just different mechanisms to deliver that transformation
to different people at different friction points. Each product strengthens
the others. Together they compound.

This skill takes a user from "I have a solution" to "I have an ecosystem
of products that reinforce each other" — by systematically mapping every
friction point around their core transformation.

## When to use

- "How do I expand beyond my one product?"
- "What else could I build around this?"
- "I want to create an ecosystem, not just a product"
- "How do I build leverage?"
- "What products should I build next?"
- "I feel like I'm leaving money on the table"
- "How do I think in systems instead of single offers?"
- Any variant of "I have one thing working, what's next"

## Prerequisite

The user needs a **working core solution** — something they've already
delivered or proven. If they don't have one yet, route them to
`problem-mining` first. This skill maps the ecosystem *around* an
existing transformation, not invents one from scratch.

## Core principle

> You're not building standalone products. You're building leverage
> mechanisms. The question isn't "what should I make?" — it's "where
> are all the friction points in this transformation, and how do I
> solve each one?"

## Process

The process has **four phases**. Complete each before advancing. One
question at a time. Each answer shapes the next question.

### Phase 1: Core transformation extraction

**Goal:** Identify the single core transformation the user delivers,
stripped of any specific product format.

Ask the user to describe what they do. Then keep stripping format
away until you reach the transformation underneath. Push until the
answer is format-agnostic.

Probe sequence:

1. "What do you help people achieve?" — get the raw answer
2. "If you couldn't use your current format (course / tool / service /
   whatever), how would you describe what you actually do?" — strip the
   format
3. "What's the before and after? What state is someone in before they
   work with you, and what state are they in after?" — clarify the
   transformation
4. "Who specifically gets this transformation? Be concrete — not
   'businesses,' but 'Series A SaaS founders doing $1-5M ARR.'" —
   nail the persona

Capture the transformation as:

```
Core transformation: [format-agnostic description]
Before state: [where the person starts]
After state: [where they end up]
Primary persona: [specific description of who]
```

**Exit criterion:** The user can state their core transformation in one
sentence without naming a product format. If they say "I sell a course
that teaches X," keep pushing — "teaching X" might be the transformation,
but the course is just a delivery mechanism.

### Phase 2: Friction point mapping

**Goal:** Map every friction point around the core transformation —
before, during, and after.

This is the engine of the whole skill. Most people only solve the
"during" (the transformation itself). The real leverage is in
everything around it.

Walk through three time horizons, one at a time:

#### Before the transformation

Ask these one at a time, adapting based on answers:

- "What is your customer doing right now that isn't working?" — the
  status quo they need to leave
- "What do they need to believe, know, or have before they're ready
  for your solution?" — prerequisites / education gaps
- "What infrastructure is missing?" — tools, systems, knowledge they
  need but don't have
- "How do they currently find and evaluate solutions like yours?" —
  discovery friction
- "What's stopping qualified people from starting?" — the gap between
  'interested' and 'committed'

#### During the transformation

- "Where do people get stuck in the middle of your process?" —
  implementation friction
- "What do they need that your core product doesn't provide?" — gaps
  in the delivery
- "What adjacent skills or tools do they need to succeed?" — things
  outside your scope that still determine their outcome
- "What support do they need that doesn't scale in your current
  model?" — support friction

#### After the transformation

- "Once someone succeeds, what problem do they hit next?" — the
  natural sequel
- "What adjacent problem does your solution create?" — second-order
  effects
- "How do they prove they succeeded?" — verification / credentialing
- "What do they need to maintain their results?" — retention /
  sustainability
- "Where do they go next, and is there friction there?" — the next
  level

Capture each friction point in a consistent format:

```
Friction point: [description]
Time horizon: [before | during | after]
Who feels it: [which persona]
Current workaround: [what they do now, if anything]
Pain level: [1-5, based on user's description]
```

**Exit criterion:** At least 3 friction points per time horizon (9+
total). If the user can only find a few, probe harder — most
ecosystems have 15-25 real friction points. But don't force imaginary
ones.

### Phase 3: Two-sided market scan

**Goal:** Identify whether another party in the ecosystem also has
friction — and whether solving both sides creates a platform
opportunity.

This is the highest-leverage move in ecosystem thinking. Most people
only see their direct customer. But there's often a second party
(employer, vendor, platform, buyer) who has complementary friction.

Ask:

- "Who else is involved in the process besides your customer?" —
  identify the other side
- "What friction does *that* party have?" — their pain
- "Would solving your customer's problem also solve theirs, or
  vice versa?" — is there a natural two-sided market
- "If both sides were on the same platform, what would that enable
  that's impossible today?" — the network effect question

If a two-sided opportunity exists, capture it:

```
Side A: [your current customer]
Side A friction: [their problem]
Side B: [the other party]
Side B friction: [their problem]
Platform opportunity: [what connecting them enables]
Network effect: [how more of A attracts B, and vice versa]
```

If no two-sided opportunity exists, that's fine — not every ecosystem
has one. Say so and move on.

**Exit criterion:** User has either identified a two-sided opportunity
or explicitly confirmed there isn't one after thorough examination.

### Phase 4: Product design and ecosystem map

**Goal:** Turn each friction point into a concrete product concept,
then map how they reinforce each other.

#### Step 1: Friction → Product

For each friction point from Phase 2 (and the two-sided opportunity
from Phase 3, if applicable), brainstorm what product could solve it.
Work through them with the user one at a time.

For each, identify:

```
Friction point: [from Phase 2]
Product concept: [what you'd build]
Format: [SaaS, course, template, marketplace, service, community, API, content, certification, tool]
Revenue model: [one-time, recurring, usage-based, commission, free-to-paid-funnel]
Effort to build: [low | medium | high]
Standalone value: [could this work on its own, or only as part of the ecosystem?]
```

#### Step 2: Reinforcement mapping

This is where ecosystem thinking beats linear thinking. For each
product, ask:

- "Which other products in the ecosystem does this make more
  valuable?" — forward links
- "Which other products make *this one* more valuable?" — backward
  links
- "Does this product generate data, users, or credibility that
  feeds another product?" — compound effects

Capture the links. The densest nodes — products that connect to
the most other products — are your highest-leverage builds.

#### Step 3: Sequence recommendation

Not everything gets built at once. Recommend a build order based on:

1. **Foundation products** — solve the most painful friction point
   and generate assets (users, data, credibility) that feed
   everything else. Build first.
2. **Amplifier products** — make existing products more valuable.
   Build second.
3. **Platform plays** — two-sided markets and infrastructure.
   Build when both sides have critical mass.
4. **Capstone products** — certification, verification, premium
   tiers. Build last, when the ecosystem has earned the authority.

## Output: Ecosystem Brief

After all four phases, produce a structured brief:

### 1. Core Transformation
One sentence. Format-agnostic.

### 2. Friction Map
Table of all friction points, organized by time horizon, with pain
levels.

### 3. Product Ecosystem
Each product concept with format, revenue model, effort, and
standalone-vs-ecosystem assessment.

### 4. Reinforcement Map
Which products strengthen which — the web of connections. Identify
the densest nodes.

### 5. Two-Sided Opportunity
If one exists: both sides, the platform concept, the network effect.

### 6. Build Sequence
Ordered list: what to build first, second, third — with rationale
for each.

### 7. Linear vs. Ecosystem Comparison
Show the user what they have now (linear) vs. what the ecosystem
looks like. Make the leverage gap concrete:

```
LINEAR (current):
  1 product → 1 revenue stream → 1 customer type

ECOSYSTEM (mapped):
  [N] products → [M] revenue streams → [K] customer types
  [X] reinforcement links between products
  [describe the compound loop]
```

## Interview style

- **One question at a time.** Same as problem-mining. Focus drives depth.
- **Use their language.** Mirror their domain terms, not MBA jargon.
- **Push for specifics.** "Some people struggle" → "Who specifically,
  and what does the struggle look like?"
- **Name the pattern.** When you spot a two-sided market or a compound
  loop, name it explicitly. The user should learn to see ecosystems,
  not just get a map.
- **Be honest about effort.** Some ecosystem products are massive
  infrastructure plays. Don't sugarcoat the build cost — sequence
  accordingly.
- **Kill weak nodes.** Not every friction point deserves a product. If
  the pain is low, the market is tiny, or it doesn't reinforce
  anything else, say so and cut it.
- **Show the compound effect.** The whole point is that 1+1=3. After
  mapping, explicitly walk through how adding product B makes product
  A more valuable, which attracts more users to product C, etc. If
  you can't articulate the loop, the ecosystem isn't real — it's just
  a product line.
