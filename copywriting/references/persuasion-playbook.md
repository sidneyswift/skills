# Persuasion playbook

Tactics beyond voice-principles, anti-slop, and formats: persuasion, positioning, story, trust, virality, and the generative process. For copy meant to sell, convert, or build an audience. Pair with voice-principles and anti-slop for the words.

## Diagnostic models

**Thrust vs. drag.** Every piece has thrust (why readers click and keep reading) and drag (meandering intros, convoluted sentences, jargon, friction of any kind). Writing spreads when thrust exceeds drag for a specific audience. Two separate problems: do you know what your audience obsesses over, and is your execution low-drag? Fix one at a time.

**The bulletproof test.** WSJ standard: poke every sentence with "what do you really mean?" Bulletproof prose holds up. Flimsy prose collapses. AI tells that fail the test: staccato rhythm, lists of three, phrases that sound specific but aren't. Run this before publishing.

**New + true + useful.** All three required before publishing. New: says something not already said. True: accurate and honest. Useful: the reader can do something with it. Missing any one kills the piece.

**The "dutiful" diagnosis.** Most bad business writing fails because the author writes like they think they're supposed to, in Wikipedia-neutral tone. The fix is structural, not motivational: if you don't care about the topic, no technique will mask it. Pick topics you actually care about.

## Persuasion architecture

**Chain of Beliefs.** Every sale is blocked by beliefs the prospect must hold before features or discounts can work. List them. Example for a note-taking course: "your brain is for having ideas, not holding them," "your ideas are worth saving," "you can get organized quickly." Then create content that installs each belief, one at a time. The chain grows as you collect objections. Content strategy = belief installation, not more tutorials.

**Hard teaching is anti-sales.** More free tutorials delay purchase: readers feel they must consume everything before paying. Replace instructional content with parable content: short stories that each deliver one insight, one shift in perspective, no to-do list. Mention the product at the end as a natural next step. Sell without selling.

**Diagnose the Before state with symptoms, not categories.** "Piles of paper everywhere," not "everything is disorganized." "Another check bounced?" not "cash flow problems." If you can describe their problem more precisely than they can, they trust you with the solution. Decision-making is emotional: no felt recognition, no action.

**Sacred vs. secular framing.** Know which value type your copy operates in. Quantifying a sacred exchange destroys it: a $5 late-pickup fine increased late pickups because it converted obligation into a cheap option. Never price friendship, identity, status, or community in dollars or metrics.

**One reader.** Steinbeck: the generalized audience doesn't exist and will scare you to death. Write to one named person, real or invented. Before drafting, write a paragraph about that reader: who they are, what they know, what they distrust, where they learn.

## Positioning and product copy

**Anchor and twist.** Position as [category the market already knows] + one differentiator. "Substack is a newsletter platform, with payments." "Superhuman is an email client that's absurdly fast." The anchor lets readers infer everything else without being told. Category creation gets blank stares; anchoring gets instant comprehension.

**Workflow stories for genuinely new products.** If no existing category fits, don't force a tidy one-liner; tidy pitches frame you as "a better X" and make novelty look incremental. Show a specific use moment instead. Early iPhone ads: watch a movie, then search for a seafood restaurant. Snapchat's best pitch: a photo of a funny-looking stranger asleep at an airport, too insignificant to text, perfect to snap.

**Storytelling beats branding for loyalty.** Branding appeals to everyone and binds no one. Story-driven companies make binding commitments that deliberately turn some people off, and the sacrifice is the proof of sincerity (Oatly: "We get it if you're not into this"). Test: would anyone tattoo your logo? Narrow passion outperforms broad appeal.

**Sell the transformation, not the tool.** Slack's 2014 memo: translate the product into what the customer becomes. The gap between Before and After is the promise; price tracks how quickly and painlessly you carry them across.

## Story structure

**Minimum viable story.** A person, in a specific place and time, wants something; they might not get it; it matters. Weak: "I worked on that project and it was successful." Strong: name the stakes, the moment, the number ("10,000 signups in the first ten minutes").

**Five Whys for stakes.** Ask "why does that matter?" until you reach stakes a peasant in rural India a thousand years ago would understand: love, death, belonging, safety. Keep the surface specific and modern; know the universal layer underneath.

**Detail calibration.** The number one story problem is too little detail; number two is too much. Choose details that serve the story's objective, not the writer's fondness for the memory.

**Named structural arcs** (reusable templates):
- Discovery Arc: friction → conventional fix fails → breakthrough → named new practice → real metrics → how to adopt it.
- Friction → Zoom Out → Framework: personal moment, broader context, usable takeaway.
- Borrowed Lens: import a concept from an unexpected discipline as the interpretive key.
- Adventure Narrative: chronological account of trying something new, driven by unexpected consequences.

**One Big Idea per piece.** Coin, claim, or crystallize exactly one nameable concept per piece ("compound engineering," "agent-native"). A topic is not an idea. If there's nothing coinable, the piece isn't ready.

## Openings and endings

**Stakes by paragraph one.** Hard rule. Open with a lived moment of friction: "I used to be physically unable to open my email." Never with a platitude: "At the end of the day, it's still just a tool."

**Ramp before the surprising claim.** A counterintuitive insight as the literal first sentence reads as abrupt. Lead with a relatable setup ("I hate to be the bearer of bad news, but the time we spent organizing our notes was probably wasted"), then land the insight two or three sentences in.

**Five opening types:** bold claim, charged anecdote (mid-scene), confessional (a mistake you made), scene-setting, manifesto declaration.

**Endings extend, never recap.** The resonance test: would a reader say "I'm not alone in feeling this"? Approved shapes: "And then I choose, one difficulty at a time." Leave the reader somewhere new.

## Voice and the AI smoothing problem

**Lectio difficilior potior: keep the harder reading.** Scribes (and AI) smooth what's weird; scholars treat the harder phrasing as the original. AI converts "altar-call energy" into "enthusiasm" and "collecting screenshots like a doomsday prepper" into "documenting examples." The weird, specific, slightly uncomfortable phrase is usually the right one. If a draft looks like AI fixed it, it has.

**Hedge-insertion is the AI tell.** "Most subscribers don't know what they're paying for" becomes "may not be fully aware of." Strip inserted hedges on revision; restore teeth.

**Voice is point of view, not style.** Style varies by format; tone belongs to the reader; voice is the constant fingerprint. It lives partly in subconscious function words (Hamilton was identified by his use of "upon"), which is why you can't fake it by swapping vocabulary. Develop voice by reacting to examples, not by self-describing.

**Coco Chanel revision rule.** Push every voice quality to maximum in a draft, wait an hour, then remove one thing. Repeat.

**Brevity signals authority.** A one-line reply to a long email signals command. Exclamation points, smileys, and corner-case-covering signal anxiety. Cutting hedges isn't rude; it's confidence.

**Embodied emotion over abstract emotion.** "The relief felt as physical as the dread had been," not "I felt relieved." Locate feelings in the body.

**Write from inside the transformation.** Participant who is experimenting and discovering, not pundit looking down. Show the mess: failures, false starts, honest limits. Vulnerability is analytical; use personal stakes to frame insights.

## Idea quality and testing

**CRIBS.** Test ideas in conversation before drafting and listen for: Confusing, Repeated, Interesting, Boring, Surprising. Chase Interesting and Surprising; drop the rest.

**Surprise is information.** Shannon: more surprise, more information. Boring writing is literally low-information. An essay should meander toward surprise (Paul Graham).

**Picasso Bull compression.** Start hyperdetailed, add complexity through research, then reverse: subtract toward essential lines. Simplicity on the far side of complexity, never instead of it.

**Test snippets before essays.** Tweet the half-formed idea; the response to a one-liner predicts the essay's reception better than months of private drafting. James Clear: atomic ideas tested individually on social, assembled into larger work only after they survive.

**Capture contradictions, not confirmations.** Notes that agree with what you already believe produce boring writing. Save friction.

## Trust mechanics

**Trust is the basis of competition.** Readers can't assess quality before reading, so they buy on signals: author brand, where it appeared, who recommended it. Essays must justify the reader's commitment up front; news headlines already gave away 90%.

**The trust thermocline.** Trust erodes non-linearly. Micro-infractions (overpromises, weasel words, fake urgency) accumulate invisibly in the shared trust commons, then break for many readers at once. Growth metrics look fine until the collapse. Every piece of copy is a deposit or a withdrawal.

**Public accuracy audits.** Armstrong scores every prediction yearly on a 1-20 scale and publishes the results (2023: 18.5 average; below 9 = quit the newsletter). Grading your own past claims publicly builds more trust than any expertise claim.

**Identity lock.** Content that reinforces who the reader is sticks harder than content that explains what a product does. Readers stay in content subcultures without feeling locked in: "it feels like living your life."

**Edgy vs. excellent.** Borderline content reliably outperforms short-term (a contrarian post earned 45x the subscriptions of a careful pricing guide), but professionals get too few shots to win the edge arms race. Compete on excellence; treat edge as occasional, not strategy.

## Audience strategy

**Match difficulty to audience.** Simple sentences attract broad, casual readers. Deliberate difficulty and insider language filter for invested experts who form deeper attachments. Sentence clarity and idea density are separate dials; top 1% requires both set right.

**Feed obsessions, don't pander.** Deep readers are cultivating an obsession. Writers chasing trends can't go deep enough to feed it. Write from your own obsession; the depth is the moat.

**Distribution outlasts content.** Hits are rentals; an owned audience (email list) is the asset. Build the permission base before the product; it doubles as proof of demand. Growth rate matters more than take rate.

**Tour other audiences.** Guest posts, podcasts, and collabs are the internet version of a band touring: visit cozy cafes (small newsletters) and arenas (big channels) instead of waiting to be discovered.

## Review personas

Run drafts past three readers in your head (or as separate AI passes):
- **Sorkin:** keeps it walking and talking; cuts anything mired in unnecessary detail.
- **Mom:** flags where a non-expert gets lost.
- **Asshole:** attacks the weakest version of the argument, which is sometimes the version you wrote.

## Virality and attention mechanics

**Psychological packaging beats capability.** ChatGPT wasn't a technical leap (the models had been API-available since 2018); it wrapped existing capability in a familiar chat interface. NotebookLM had the same features for a year; visits went parabolic only after the podcast-generation feature. When launching, the question is not "what can it do" but "what psychological trigger does the packaging fire." Rory Sutherland: the next revolution may be psychological, not technological.

**Virality is partly cascade luck.** AI persona simulations (1,903 cloned Hacker News users) predict viral headlines at only 60% accuracy, because one early upvote sends identical content down completely different paths. Implication: treat virality as a portfolio game with many shots, not a craft guarantee; and pre-test headlines on simulated audiences anyway, since 60% beats 50% meaningfully at scale. A persona given a 2-hour interview brief matches human survey answers at 85%. Use many personas, not one model: a single AI gives the averaged textbook answer; 100 personas give a distribution. Give one persona a harsh-critic brief to defeat default AI politeness.

**Attention has shape and quality.** It can be narrowed (more sensitive), widened, filtered, held in reserve; its quality ranges sharp, diffuse, warm, cold. What you direct the reader's attention to grows in their experience; that choice is the core creative act of copy. Holding attention requires constant change: "the effort to capture it has to be constantly changing, constantly tracking the changing mood."

**Stream vs. garden.** Stream content (takes, posts) is ephemeral participation; garden content (frameworks, guides, "thingified ideas" that stand alone) compounds and can earn money on its own. Ideas gain value as they age only in gardens. Everyone wants to play in the stream; those who build gardens win.

## Readiness and influence

**The stoplight model.** Direct pitches land only when the prospect's light is green (already talking about wanting change). Most people are red most of the time, and pitching at red backfires. CRAFT-style indirect influence (helping people connect with their own desire to change) succeeds ~70% vs ~30% for confrontation. Copy sequencing: shift readers toward green first (belief installation, parables, problem mirroring), pitch second.

**Make incentive alignment visible.** "Companies that profit from doing what's in customers' interest outcompete those that profit from what customers dislike." Sharpest version: expose the incumbent's misalignment with their own numbers (Warby Parker vs. $15-to-make, $800-to-buy glasses; even LensCrafters' founder called it "a complete rip-off"). State your own alignment plainly: "we only make money when you X." Align messaging with every actor in the value chain, not just the buyer.

**Name the underserved audience that doesn't expect it.** Telling ambitious founders they're an "underserved population" produces surprise plus strong recognition. Template: high-status audience + unacknowledged private struggle + the word "underserved." Related: position as ceiling-raising (making capable people exceptional), not floor-raising (fixing dysfunction); ceiling-raising is the neglected, premium frame. The "beyond X, actually Y" structure reframes generic category advice: "beyond realistic goals, honesty about long odds."

**Demonstrate value instead of applying.** The crash-pitch move: skip the resume/cover-letter format everyone uses; send the specific company your analysis and proposal. Two requirements: provide value, and show you can provide value. Works for jobs, partnerships, and cold outreach alike. Related: resumes are myth-making with zero predictive power; the hidden struggle is the interesting part, so acknowledge and subvert the polished format when pitching.

## Generative process (getting words out)

**Madman before Judge.** Two energies compete: the Madman (generates, ten pages an hour) and the Judge (educated, critical, "can't create anything"). Blocks happen when the Judge runs first. Sequence them; never run both at once. If you can't make writing, "just make it typing." More words down = better odds the right ones are among them.

**First thought = first image.** Scan prompts until one fires a solid visual image from the physical world; start there, not from an abstraction. The image is the seed.

**The unstuck loop.** Brainstorm 10 big-concept keywords; free-associate 5 minutes on each; take the richest; write 5 questions from it; mine one word or phrase into another freewrite; loop until an idea you could write about for a long time surfaces. Variants: invisible freewriting (dim the screen so you can't self-edit); write "I can't think of anything" until content surfaces; Particle/Wave/Field (is the topic a thing, a process, or a system of relationships?); Look In / Look Out / Look Forward. Don't write to prove something; write to learn something.

**Generative questions that reliably open pieces:** What do I experience regularly that others might not? What would I write if I could publish anonymously? If one named person were guaranteed to read this, what would I write? What seems obvious to me that isn't obvious to others? What do I accept as true that most people doubt?

**Mind-wandering is the engine.** Creativity science: idea generation runs on the brain's default network, which forced focus shuts off. Incubation is irreplaceable; schedule input, then walks, not more screen time. Semantic memory richness predicts creative output, so feeding the notebook is training. Capture contradictions, typos, misheard lines; decontextualized fragments generate new meaning. Re-reading your own old writing is a creative act.

## Intros and endings, refined

**Intro rhythm:** spark of the idea on top, stakes established within 150 words, quick zoom out, thesis pointing forward. Not background-problem-thesis.

**Abstraction must be grounded.** Abstract claims pass the bar only when anchored in specific, sensory, or narrative detail.

**A recap is a photocopy; a reframe is a second exposure.** Endings shift the angle of view on the same material.

**Three voice principles:** optimistic realism (never cynicism), intellectual generosity (argue in a way that invites people in), conversational authority (confidence coexisting with humility). Style is the how (syntax, rhythm, imagery); voice is the why (the convictions underneath). Rules alone describe a style; a voice requires encoded values.

## Submissions and pitches (procedural)

Most rejections are procedural, not quality-based: follow the guidelines exactly (submissions are judged against a consistent baseline; "innovating the format" reads as carelessness), respect word limits, read the publication until you can describe its aesthetic, keep the bio short (it's a writing sample too), and treat "we enjoyed your work, submit again" as a real signal, not boilerplate.

## Pre-publication checklist

- Stakes clear by paragraph one?
- Lived moment of friction in the opening?
- Real evidence shown (screenshots, numbers, named examples)?
- All technical terms ELI5'd?
- Clear transformation (before → after)?
- Ending extends the idea (not summarizes)?
- Would someone say "I'm not alone in feeling this"?

Build your own checklist from observed failures, not aspirations: if your drafts keep producing tidy summary endings, add "does the ending preserve energy?"
