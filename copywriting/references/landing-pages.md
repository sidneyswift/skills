# Landing page and conversion architecture

This reference governs the shape and order of conversion-focused pages: homepages, landing pages, pricing pages, feature pages, and product pages. The words themselves still follow [voice-principles.md](voice-principles.md) and [anti-slop.md](anti-slop.md). Use this for structure. Use those for craft. Persuasion strategy (the beliefs a buyer must hold, positioning, trust) lives in [persuasion-playbook.md](persuasion-playbook.md); this is the page-mechanics layer that sits on top of it.

The one test that overrides every template here: if you can swap in a competitor's name and the copy still reads true, it is too generic. Rewrite until it only fits this product.

## How the voice rules apply to pages

Page copy tempts you toward slop more than any other format. Three reconciliations:

1. **Question headlines are allowed. Reframe questions are not.** "Still chasing invoices by email?" is a fine hook: it names a real pain. "Is this a pricing problem? No, it is a positioning problem." is banned by the anti-slop reframe rule. A question that names a pain passes. A question that rejects one frame to install another fails.
2. **"Stop X. Start Y." is banned here.** The conversion world loves "Stop chasing invoices. Start getting paid." It is the negative-parallelism pattern anti-slop forbids. Write the positive claim directly: "Get paid the day the work ships." Same for "Less X, more Y" and "X is dead. Y is the future."
3. **Analogies and similes still need permission.** A headline like "Market like a Fortune 500" is a simile. Keep it only if it passes the 5-part permission test in anti-slop.md. Otherwise state the outcome literally.

And the global rule: no em dashes anywhere, including headlines and CTAs. Recast with a period, comma, colon, or parentheses.

## Headline formulas

The headline carries one message: what this does, for whom. Pick a formula by what you are leading with, then fill it with something specific enough that a competitor could not reuse it.

### Lead with the outcome

- **{Achieve outcome} without {pain point}** > "Ship the weekly report without the Sunday spreadsheet"
- **{Achieve outcome} by {how it works}** > "Generate leads by seeing which companies visit your site"
- **Turn {input} into {outcome}** > "Turn one-time buyers into repeat customers"
- **{Outcome} in {timeframe}** > "Get your refund in 10 days"

### Lead with the problem

- **Never {unpleasant event} again** > "Never miss a renewal again"
- **{Question naming the main pain}** > "Still chasing invoices by email?"

### Lead with the audience

- **{Category} for {audience}** > "Analytics for Shopify stores"
- **{Category} for {audience} to {job}** > "A whiteboard for remote teams to plan a sprint"
- **You don't need {skill or resource} to {outcome}** > "You don't need an SEO team to rank"

### Lead with the difference

- **The {category} that {differentiator}** > "The CRM that updates itself"
- **The {opposite of the usual process} way to {outcome}** > "The no-code way to launch a store"

### Lead with proof

- **{Number} {people} use {product} to {outcome}** > "50,000 marketers use it to send better emails"
- **Finally, {category} that {benefit}** > "Finally, scheduling that respects time zones"

### Use with care

- **{Action verb} your {thing} like {admirable example}** > "Market like a Fortune 500." A simile. Only if it clears the analogy permission test.
- **What if you could {outcome}?** A question headline. Fine as a hook, never as a reframe.

## CTA copy

State the action and what they get. The verb is concrete; the object is the value, not the mechanic.

- Weak (avoid): Submit, Sign up, Learn more, Click here, Get started
- Strong (use): Start free trial, Get the checklist, See pricing for my team, Create your first report

Formula: **[action verb] + [what they get] + [qualifier if needed]**. "Start my free trial" beats "Get started today." One primary action per page. Repeat the same CTA rather than offering a menu.

## The section library

A page is a sequence of sections that tells one argument and answers objections in order. Core sections do the work; supporting sections are added when the buyer needs them.

### Core sections

- **Hero (above the fold):** headline, subhead (one specific proof point or differentiator, 1-2 sentences), primary CTA, one supporting visual. Optionally a thin social-proof bar.
- **Social proof bar:** recognizable logos beat many logos. One hard metric ("10,000 teams"), a rating with a review count, or one specific testimonial line.
- **Problem:** describe their situation more precisely than they can, so they think "that is exactly my situation." Name the cost of leaving it unsolved.
- **Solution and benefits:** 3 to 5 benefits, not 10. Each one is a headline (the outcome) plus 1-2 sentences (how it works) plus proof when you have it.
- **How it works:** 3 to 4 numbered steps. Each step is a plain verb plus the outcome of that step. This lowers perceived complexity.
- **Final CTA:** restate the value in one line, repeat the primary CTA, add risk reversal (free trial, guarantee, cancel anytime).

### Supporting sections (add when the buyer needs them)

- **Testimonials:** full quotes with name, role, company. Specific results over praise.
- **Case study snippet:** problem, solution, result with a real number, and a "read more" link.
- **Use cases or "built for" blocks:** let visitors self-identify ("For finance teams who close the books monthly").
- **FAQ:** the 5 to 10 real objections, answered plainly. Doubles as SEO.
- **Comparison:** versus a named competitor or versus the status quo (the spreadsheet, the manual process). Table or side by side.
- **Integrations:** logos of what you connect to. "Works with your stack."
- **Founder note or manifesto:** why this exists, what you believe. Use when the category is crowded and belief is the differentiator.
- **Demo or product tour:** video, interactive, or a short clip that shows the product doing the thing.
- **Pricing preview:** a "from $X per month" teaser, even on non-pricing pages, to move decision-makers forward.
- **Guarantee or risk reversal:** the terms that remove the last hesitation.
- **Stats:** a few credibility numbers (customers, rating, dollars saved).

## Page templates by type

Order is an argument. A flat list of features is the weakest possible page:

```
Hero > Feature 1 > Feature 2 > Feature 3 > Feature 4 > CTA
```

That is a list, not a case. Use one of these instead.

**Full page (homepage or primary landing):**
```
1. Hero with a clear value prop
2. Social proof bar
3. Problem
4. How it works (3 steps)
5. Key benefits (2 to 3)
6. Testimonial
7. Use cases or personas
8. Comparison to the alternative
9. Case study snippet
10. FAQ
11. Final CTA with guarantee
```

**Compact (ad or campaign landing, where brevity matters):**
```
1. Hero (headline, subhead, CTA, visual)
2. Social proof bar
3. 3 key benefits
4. Testimonial
5. How it works (3 steps)
6. Final CTA with guarantee
```

**Enterprise and B2B:**
```
1. Hero (outcome-focused headline)
2. Logo bar (recognizable companies)
3. Problem (business pain)
4. Solution overview
5. Use cases by role or department
6. Security and compliance
7. Integrations
8. Case study with metrics
9. ROI or value section
10. Contact or demo CTA
```

**Product launch:**
```
1. Hero with the launch announcement
2. Video demo
3. Feature highlights (3 to 5)
4. Before and after
5. Early testimonials
6. Launch or early-access pricing
7. CTA with a real deadline
```

## Writing the key sections

**Problem.** Do not open with a cliche ("You know the feeling," "We have all been there"). That is the throat-clearing voice-principles tells you to cut. Open with the specific symptom, in their words: "Three tools, and the numbers still do not match on Friday." Then name the time, money, or risk it costs.

**Benefits.** For each: a headline that states the outcome, 1-2 sentences on how it happens, and one proof point when you have a real one. Translate features into outcomes. "Automated invoicing" becomes "Invoices go out the day the work ships."

**How it works.** Number the steps. Each is a plain verb plus the result. "Connect your tools (2 minutes). Set your preferences. Get a report every Monday."

**Testimonial selection.** Keep the ones with a specific result ("cut reporting from 4 hours to 15 minutes"), a before and after, and a real name, role, and company. Cut "Great product" and "Love it."

## Output habit

When you deliver page copy:

- Organize it by section, in page order.
- For the headline and the primary CTA, give 2 to 3 options, each with a one-line reason.
- Annotate only the non-obvious choices with the principle behind them.
- Include the page title and meta description when the page is public.
