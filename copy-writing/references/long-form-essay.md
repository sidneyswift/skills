# Long-form essay architecture

This reference covers essay-specific structure. The universal voice principles (tone, sentence patterns, specificity, evidence, openings, endings) are in [voice-principles.md](voice-principles.md) and apply to all writing. Load this reference when writing essays, articles, or thought pieces of 1,000+ words. See "Scaling by length" below to fit the blueprint to your word count.

---

## Scaling by length

The full 7-section blueprint below targets 4,000 to 8,000 words. Shorter pieces use the same structure with less depth. Compress, don't pad:

- **1,000-2,000 words:** hook and thesis, one evidence section, the argument in 2-3 steps, one paragraph of counterargument, a resonant ending. Skip the philosophical extension and the evocative register.
- **2,000-4,000 words:** add the dedicated counterargument section and one extended example.
- **4,000+ words:** the full blueprint below.

Keep the section order. Cut depth, not structure. Below 1,000 words this is the wrong reference; use the format rules in [formats.md](formats.md) instead.

## Essay architecture (7-section blueprint)

### 1. The paradox / hook (~500 words)

State a counterintuitive observation. Ground it immediately in operational specifics. Build tension with evidence that the conventional wisdom is wrong. End with the thesis stated plainly.

Example flow:
- Open: "There is a paradox at the heart of AI."
- Evidence: "We've automated everything we can. And yet there's more human work to do than ever."
- Conventional wisdom: Quotes from Amodei, Griffin, benchmark numbers
- Thesis: "There's no tipping point coming where things flip and the jobs are gone."

### 2. Credibility / "How we got here" (~300 words)

Brief history establishing track record. References prior predictions that played out. Purpose: earn the right to make big claims later.

> "Three years ago, I wrote about the allocation economy. This was back when basic prompts inside ChatGPT were still considered alarmingly futuristic."

### 3. The landscape / evidence tour (~1,500 words)

Detailed tour of current reality. This is the evidence base for everything that follows.

Requirements:
- Named people with roles (Kieran Klaassen, general manager of Cora)
- Named tools and products (Codex, Claude Code, Fin, Proof)
- Specific numbers (65 percent of 202 conversations, $62 per deck, 24 skills and 18 scripts)
- Screenshots or visual descriptions when relevant
- Organize with clear subsections (each subsection = one aspect of the landscape)

### 4. The argument / "Why" (~1,500 words)

The analytical core. Chain of reasoning where each step builds on the evidence from section 3.

Structure: use sub-headers as stepping stones. Each header advances the argument one step:
- "AI makes yesterday's human competence cheap"
- "Cheap competence gets rapidly adopted"
- "Abundance creates sameness"
- "Sameness creates demand for difference"
- "Demand for difference is new demand for experts"

Each subsection: 200-400 words. Claim + evidence + implication. The evidence comes from section 3.

### 5. The counterargument (~1,500 words)

Name the strongest objection explicitly. Spend real time on it — not a dismissive paragraph.

Structure:
1. State the objection clearly ("But what about exponential benchmark progress?")
2. Show the evidence behind it (benchmark numbers, headlines, real results)
3. Go deep into *how* that evidence was produced (e.g., dissect an actual benchmark prompt)
4. Demonstrate why the evidence, properly understood, actually supports your thesis
5. Emerge with a stronger version of your claim

This section often contains the essay's best analytical work. It's where named concepts often get introduced (e.g., "chart psychosis," "smuggled intelligence").

### 6. The philosophical extension (~1,000 words)

Push the argument into deeper territory. This is where the essay earns its keep as "thought piece."

May include:
- Definitions and distinctions (e.g., "frame vs. framer," "competence vs. situated taste")
- Unexpected conceptual connections
- The Zeno's paradox structure (showing why the gap is structural, not temporary)

This section only works after 4,000+ words of evidence. Don't attempt it early.

### 7. The resonant ending (~500 words)

Three-part pattern:
1. Short philosophical restatement (~100 words)
2. An unexpected image or sensory passage (~200 words) — present tense, second person, vivid
3. A literary coda (~200 words) — story from a completely different domain that mirrors the argument without restating it

Example: The rabbi's story about the man who could find everything except himself.

## Extended example technique

One section of the essay (usually within the counterargument) goes deep on a single case study. 500+ words on one example.

Include:
- How it was built / how it works
- What it tests or demonstrates
- The actual prompt or input (quoted)
- Specific scores or results
- What happens when you vary the inputs
- What this tells us about the broader argument

This is not a brief illustration. It's an analytical deep-dive that does real argumentative work.

---

## Section connections

Sections don't use transition words. They use transition structures:

- A new header picks up where the previous section left off conceptually
- The first sentence of a new section echoes the last idea of the previous one
- Direct bridges: "You might already see, in the midst of all of this automation, where the humans come in."
- Short transition paragraphs: "But there is a second-order reason, too."

---

## Evocative register (essay-only)

Near the end, after the argument is complete, the essay shifts into a more sensory, present-tense register:

> "You sit and watch the tokens stream in hypnotically. And you begin to imagine yourself running in this race, too, a ghost of yourself superimposed onto the track."

> "A ghost chasing a ghost, and winning."

Rules:
- Only appears after 4,000+ words of analytical writing
- Uses present tense, second person ("you")
- 2-3 passages maximum per essay
- The contrast with the analytical voice is what makes it land
- If you haven't earned it with evidence, it reads as purple prose

---

## Footnotes and marginalia

Use numbered notes for:
- Technical caveats that interrupt flow
- Self-aware qualifications ("I like this definition because it's measurable")
- Comparisons and context that enrich but aren't load-bearing
- Attribution for borrowed concepts

Keep the main argument clean. Marginalia is the writer's side-channel for curious readers.

---

## Word count targets

- Total: 4,000–8,000 words
- Sections 3-4 (evidence + argument) should be the longest combined block (~3,000 words)
- Opening and closing should be tight (500 words each)
- Counterargument section is substantial (1,000-1,500 words)

---

## Common mistakes in long-form

1. **Over-naming concepts.** One named concept per essay. Not 3-4 — that's a brand exercise.
2. **Skipping the evidence.** Abstract reasoning without operational specifics is not this style.
3. **Rushing the philosophical pivot.** Don't go philosophical before word 4,000.
4. **Dismissing the counterargument.** One paragraph is not enough. 1,000+ words.
5. **Making the ending too neat.** If it "wraps up" cleanly, it's wrong. Leave the reader thinking.
6. **Uniform paragraph length.** The power is in variation. Short next to long.
7. **Being too casual.** Warm and accessible but not colloquial. No slang, memes, internet speak.
