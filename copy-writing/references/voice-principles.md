# Voice principles

These principles shape all writing — social posts, emails, essays, landing pages, everything. They are the default voice. Apply with judgment; not every principle fits every format, but the instincts should be present.

---

## Lead with the counterintuitive or surprising

Don't open with context-setting. Open with the thing that makes someone stop scrolling.

> "There is a paradox at the heart of AI."

> "Time isn't as linear as you think."

> "Here's a question: Are we officially in the part of the movie where human experts lose their livelihoods?"

The first sentence earns the second. If the opening could be swapped into any other post on the same topic, it's too generic.

For social posts, this means the first line IS the hook. For emails, it means the subject line or first sentence. For essays, it's the thesis paragraph. The scale changes, the principle doesn't.

---

## Concrete before abstract

Every claim needs a specific example nearby. The example can come before or after, but never let an idea float without evidence.

- Weak: "AI creates more work for humans."
- Strong: "Operations people are writing code and issuing pull requests. Marketers are making YouTube thumbnails. Engineers are writing drafts of articles." → then the claim.

For a LinkedIn post, this means one real example in the first 3 lines. For a landing page, it means a specific number or customer result above the fold. For an essay, it means every analytical paragraph has a concrete anchor within 100 words.

### The evidence stack

When listing examples, use parallel structure with escalating specificity:

> "We didn't just automate email. We automated proposals, training decks, project tracking, and newsletter drafts."

Three items minimum. The last one is the most specific. Same grammatical form throughout.

---

## Earned confidence

Take a stance. State the basis. No hedging into nothing, no overselling into noise.

- Good: "I believe there are strong technological forces keeping AI a few steps behind human experts."
- Too hedged: "AI might possibly face some challenges in fully replacing workers."
- Too absolute: "AI will never replace humans. Period."

The tone is: I've thought about this seriously, here's where I landed, here's why.

Plain uncertainty when genuinely uncertain: "I think," "probably," "my guess is." These are honest. What's banned is performative hedging — qualifying every sentence until the stance disappears.

---

## Specificity as proof

Numbers, names, dates, tools, prices, percentages, time periods. These are not decoration — they're what makes claims believable.

> "For the last several weeks, AI has responded to 95 percent of my work emails."

> "One of our PowerPoint automations includes 24 skills and 18 scripts and costs $62 in tokens to make a single deck."

> "In a recent week in May, Fin participated in 65 percent of 202 support conversations and closed 81 of them without a human."

In a social post, one specific number can carry the entire hook. In a case study, layer them. In a cold email, one specific result replaces three paragraphs of description.

---

## First-person operational authority

Write from inside the work, not about it. You earn the right to make claims by showing the specific details of how the work actually happens.

> "I composed this piece in Proof in the in-app browser of Codex."

is stronger than:

> "Tools like Codex enable a new writing workflow."

This applies at every scale:
- **Social post:** "We shipped X last week. Here's what happened." (not "Companies should consider X.")
- **Email:** "I saw your team does Y. We do something similar." (not "Many teams face the challenge of Y.")
- **Essay:** "At our company, we've automated everything we can. And yet..." (not "Companies that adopt AI find that...")

If you haven't done the thing, don't pretend. Cite someone who has.

---

## Named concepts as compression handles

When a multi-sentence idea keeps recurring, compress it into a 2-3 word phrase. This works at every scale — a LinkedIn post can introduce one named concept and make it the entire point.

Rules:
1. The name must make the idea easier to think about, not just catchier
2. It should be graspable on first encounter ("chart psychosis" — you immediately get it)
3. It must do explanatory work — you can use it to analyze other situations
4. Introduce it *after* showing the phenomenon, not before
5. One per piece. Two max in long-form. Zero if nothing compresses naturally.
6. Don't force it. A plainly described idea beats a forced coinage.

Examples: "allocation economy," "chart psychosis," "smuggled intelligence," "human sandwich."

---

## Sentence variety as rhythm

Mix lengths deliberately. Monotone rhythm — whether all short or all long — makes writing feel robotic.

- Short declarative sentences to land a point: "The paradox remains."
- Medium sentences for evidence and context (the workhorse, ~55% of sentences)
- Longer sentences for complex reasoning, but only when the complexity is genuine

### The one-sentence paragraph

Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis, not as a default rhythm. They punctuate — they don't dominate.

> "And yet, the paradox remains."

> "But there is a second-order reason, too."

In a LinkedIn post (shorter format), maybe 1-2 of these. In a 5,000 word essay, one per 400-600 words. If every paragraph is one sentence, you've created a different kind of slop.

---

## Parenthetical personality

Parenthetical asides add personality, self-awareness, or a side-channel thought. They're the writer thinking aloud.

> "(Next stop: chart psychosis.)"

> "(an extremely rare state for me)"

> "(I also have two rewrites from human senior engineers, who used AI, that I use to compare and grade the model output.)"

Rules:
- 1-2 per social post (max)
- 2-4 per essay
- 0 per cold email (too casual)
- They should feel like the writer couldn't help but add the aside — not like a comedy bit

---

## Acknowledge the reader's position

Name what the reader is likely feeling or thinking, without pandering or dismissing.

> "We watch the benchmarks and sweat, wondering if there's a tipping point around the corner."

> "The first time you see the capabilities of a new model you are floored — and probably a little scared. But a few months later they feel ordinary."

This works for anxiety, skepticism, excitement, or confusion. Meet the reader where they are, sit with it for a sentence, then redirect toward your point. One sentence of acknowledgment. Not three paragraphs.

---

## Source integration

Attribute naturally. Lead with the person or the claim, not the citation apparatus.

- Good: "Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei warns that AI could wipe out up to half of entry-level white-collar jobs."
- Bad: "According to a recent report by Anthropic (2026), it has been suggested that..."

For self-reference: natural and direct. "I wrote about this last year" — not "In my previous article, I posited..."

---

## The steelman habit

Before arguing your point, state the best version of the opposing view. Not a straw man. The real objection a thoughtful reader would raise.

In a social post: one sentence acknowledging the counterargument, then your response.
In an essay: an entire section (500+ words) taking the objection seriously.

> "The obvious objection: Look at the exponential benchmark gains and all of this is temporary — just wait for the models to catch up."

Then absorb it into your thesis rather than dismissing it. The reader should feel like you've thought harder about the counterargument than they have.

---

## Endings that open, not close

Don't summarize. Don't restate. Don't end with a call to action unless the format requires it (ads, landing pages).

For social posts: end with the most specific or surprising detail, not a general conclusion. Or end with a question that's genuinely open.

For essays: shift register — a story, an image, or a passage from a completely different domain that refracts the argument. The reader makes the connection.

For emails: end with the specific next step. Not "Let me know your thoughts."

The instinct across all formats: the last sentence should make the reader think, not just nod.

---

## Tone calibration

### What this voice IS
- Confident but not dogmatic
- Curious but not hedging
- Warm but not chatty (no slang, no memes)
- Smart but not academic
- Direct but not aggressive

### What this voice IS NOT
- Not sarcastic or snarky
- Not breathless tech hype ("This changes everything!")
- Not performatively humble ("I'm just a simple founder...")
- Not newsletter-bro ("Let me break this down for you")
- Not academic ("This paper argues that...")
- Not apocalyptic (even when discussing alarming trends)

### Emotional register
Acknowledge emotion without amplifying it. Name what readers feel, sit with it for one sentence, then redirect toward analysis. Never exploit anxiety. Never dismiss it.
